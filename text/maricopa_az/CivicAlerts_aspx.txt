Father Gets Death Penalty for Christmas Day Massacre

Anthony Milan Ross killed his ex-wife and their two kids.

(PHOENIX) – Maricopa County Attorney Rachel Mitchell announced today that Anthony Milan Ross (12/1971) has been sentenced to death for the premeditated murders of his ex-wife and their two children.

On December 25, 2017, Ross’ ex-wife, Iris, went to his apartment near 16th Street and Highland Avenue to pick up their kids from him to celebrate Christmas Day. Once she arrived at Ross’ apartment’s parking lot, she and Ross discussed their failed marriage. After Iris had told Ross that she would not take him back, he shot her multiple times as she tried to run away. He then stood over her and shot her additional times after she had fallen to the ground. 

Shortly after, Ross went into his apartment, where he turned the gun on 8-month-old Anora, killing her, and then shot and killed 11-year-old Nigel, who put up his hand in an attempt to defend himself from his father. Ross then returned to the parking lot, where two witnesses saw him going through Iris’ pants pockets. They asked him if she was ok. Ross said no, pointed a gun at them, and went back into his apartment after the witnesses had fled from him in terror.  

Phoenix Police arrived, leading to a standoff with Ross that lasted several hours, as Ross threatened to kill the children if the police were to make entry into his apartment. After several hours of police negotiations had broken down, Ross suddenly began shooting. Officers then immediately made entry into the apartment, where the defendant fired multiple gunshots at them until he ran out of ammunition. 

A jury found Ross guilty of all charges:

three counts of First-Degree Murder, a Class One Dangerous Felony
two counts of Aggravated Assault, a Class Three Dangerous Felony
18 counts of Aggravated Assault, a Class Two Dangerous Felony

In addition to today’s death sentence, Ross was given 18 years in prison for the Aggravated Assault charges.

“This defendant was supposed to protect his family; instead, he made a deliberate choice to take their lives on Christmas Day when his wife wouldn’t take him back,” said Maricopa County Attorney Rachel Mitchell. “Today's sentence reflects the gravity of what he did, and he deserves the ultimate punishment. Thanks to prosecutors Kirsten Valenzuela, Jordan Uglietta, and Richard Dusterhoft, there is justice for Iris, Nigel, and Anora.”

Lanunziata and the victim, Joseph Lephiew, lived together for approximately one year prior to the shooting. 

When officers arrived, Lanunziata exited the residence through a second-story window. He spoke with first responders for several minutes from the roof of the house.  He stated there was only one gun in the house and that it belonged to him. Officers entered the residence and found the victim, Joseph Lephiew, lying on the stairs with gunshot wounds. He was unarmed, and there was no evidence of any weapons near his body. He was pronounced dead at the scene. 

Officers located Lanunziata’s handgun at the top of the staircase along with shell casings.

Other residents in the home reported hearing an argument followed by multiple gunshots. Evidence confirmed the shooting took place on the staircase. Lanunziata claimed the victim “came at him” and asserted he acted in self-defense. Inconsistent statements from Lanunziata, who manufactured a reality to fit his claim, proved the shooting was not in self-defense. 

Lanunziata was charged and convicted on one Count of Second-Degree Murder, a Class One Dangerous Felony. 

“This was not self-defense—it was a deadly and unjustified act of violence inside a place where people should have felt safe,” said County Attorney Rachel Mitchell. “The defendant chose to bring a gun into that home, escalate a conflict, and take a life. 2026 Marks 45 Years of National Crime Victims’ Rights Week

Honoring victims and the professionals who support them.

(PHOENIX) – Every year, the Maricopa County Attorney’s Office (MCAO) provides critical support to thousands of crime victims in Maricopa County. This week, MCAO joins agencies across the state and country in observance of the 45th Anniversary of National Crime Victims’ Rights Week by raising awareness of the importance of victims’ rights and the impact they have on criminal proceedings.

“Every victim has a story that matters. Victims are at the center of everything we do here at MCAO. Our role is to make a positive impact during one of the worst times in a victim’s life,” said Maricopa County Attorney Rachel Mitchell. “It’s an honor to be there for victims, and this week reminds us of the role we all play in helping them start their healing journey.”

MCAO is committed to protecting and upholding the rights of crime victims through support, communication, and advocacy. Last year, advocates in the Victim Services Division (VSD) assisted 22,350 victims while working on 11,679 cases. VSD specialists also assisted 730 victims in processing restitution court orders totaling $7,402,052. Restitution is a court-ordered payment process from the offender to the victim that restores the loss caused by the offense.

In addition to victim advocates and other specialists, MCAO’s VSD also includes a facility dog named Coco who accompanies victims during interviews, case-related meetings and trials.

Today, at the 2026 Arizona Award Ceremony for National Crime Victims’ Rights Week, Victim Advocate Melissa Molina was recognized for the lasting impact she’s had on countless victims. Melissa joined MCAO in May of 2018 and is part of the Survivor Advocacy and Support Bureau which focuses on supporting those who have lost a loved one to violent crime.

“Helping victims of crime is meaningful to me because it allows me to provide compassionate support, gentle guidance, and a safe place for victims and survivors who are going through an incredibly difficult time,” said Melissa Molina. “I value being able to see the impact it makes on a victim when they feel seen and supported.”

For more information about victims’ rights, visit our National Crime Victims’ Rights Week webpage.

Maricopa County Attorney's Office Launches Awareness Campaign to Combat Human Trafficking

(PHOENIX, AZ) – The Maricopa County Attorney’s Office (MCAO) is launching an awareness campaign titled “Break the Chain” to teach communities what they need to know about human trafficking and how to report it.

“Unlike many other crimes, human trafficking is not easy to notice,” said County Attorney Rachel Mitchell. “Traffickers are master manipulators and will entice victims with false job offers, romantic relationships, or promises of money. Many times, victims don’t realize they are in a trafficking situation.”

Over the past several years, MCAO has seen a dramatic rise in sex trafficking cases submitted by law enforcement. In 2023, the office received 107 submittals from law enforcement related to sex trafficking. Last year, that number climbed to 172, a 60% increase. These cases often involve buyers identified through sting operations or traffickers exploiting children online.

In the area known as “The Blade,” a stretch of 27th Avenue near Indian School Road, MCAO has partnered with Phoenix Police, the Maricopa County Sheriff’s Office, Grand Canyon University Police, and other agencies to disrupt human trafficking and restore safety to this neighborhood. The “Break the Chain” campaign is also part of this effort and will feature social media, digital and TV ads to spread the word.

“Human trafficking doesn’t stay behind closed doors. It spills into people’s front yards, bringing drug trafficking, gun violence, and other violent crimes. Residents along ‘The Blade’ live with this reality every day,” said County Attorney Rachel Mitchell. “I’m asking every resident of Maricopa County to learn the signs and speak up when something doesn’t look right. My office will continue to do the necessary work to protect our community, support victims, and hold traffickers accountable.”

In addition to reporting suspected trafficking, the County Attorney’s Office encourages parents to talk early and often with their children about online safety and cyber predators. Teachers and school staff are also reminded that sex trafficking of a minor is child abuse, and mandatory reporting laws apply.

If you believe you are a victim or suspect trafficking, please contact the Arizona Human Trafficking Tipline at 1-877-4-AZTIPS (1-877-429-8477) or online at azactic.gov. When you report, be ready to describe what you saw, where and when it happened, and why it seemed suspicious.

If there is immediate danger, please call 9-1-1. Never confront a suspected trafficker or victim.

To learn more about human trafficking visit MCAO’s Break the Chain webpage.

Every case submitted to the Maricopa County Attorney’s Office is evaluated using the same standard: whether there is a “reasonable likelihood of conviction.” After careful review of the evidence submitted by Chandler PD, it was determined this case does not meet that standard. MCAO’s review of the case involved the attorneys assigned to it, along with highly experienced senior attorneys and the County Attorney herself.

Singh also pleaded guilty to one count of Interference with Monitoring Devices, a Class Four Felony for cutting off his ankle monitor about six weeks after posting bond. He was given 3.5 years of supervised probation for that offense.

"Art heists may seem glamorous in the movies, but in Maricopa County, they end with a prison sentence instead of a dramatic getaway,” said Maricopa County Attorney Rachel Mitchell. “Thanks to the quick response from Scottsdale Police and MCAO prosecutors Richelle Burch and Jonathan Hutcheson, the only art this thief will be looking at for the next few years will be whatever’s etched on the walls of his cell.”
